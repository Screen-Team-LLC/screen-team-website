Rescreens

Worn, sagging, or torn screening brought back to clean — fast, reliable, and done right the first time.

What Is a Rescreen?

Over time, screen material on pool cages, lanais, porches, and patio enclosures takes a beating from Florida's sun, humidity, storms, and wildlife. It tears, sags, oxidizes, or simply wears out and stops doing its job.

A rescreen is exactly what it sounds like — the old screening is removed and replaced with fresh material, re-splined into the existing frame. When the frame is still solid, a rescreen is the most cost-effective way to get your enclosure looking and functioning like new again.

What We Replace

  • Pool cage and pool enclosure screening
  • Lanai and porch screen panels
  • Patio and outdoor room screens
  • Individual torn or damaged panels
  • Full enclosure rescreens when the frame is sound

Signs You Need a Rescreen

  • Visible tears, holes, or rips in the screen material
  • Sagging or loose panels that won't stay taut
  • Screen pulling away from the frame or spline
  • Discolored, oxidized, or deteriorating mesh
  • Bugs getting in despite the enclosure being "closed"

Rescreen FAQs

Straight answers about rescreening in Tampa Bay.

Rescreening replaces only the screen mesh while keeping the existing aluminum frame. A full replacement means removing and rebuilding the frame structure. If the frame is still solid and square, a rescreen is significantly more affordable and is typically the right call.

We can rescreen select panels only. No need to replace material that's still in good shape. Chris will assess what actually needs work and quote accordingly — no upsell to a full rescreen if a partial job is the honest answer.

Most residential rescreens are completed in a single day. Larger pool cages or multi-section enclosures may take two days. You'll get a realistic timeframe when Chris reviews the job.

If the aluminum framing is straight, the posts are stable, and there's no major structural damage, a rescreen is usually the right move. Bent, cracked, or severely oxidized framing may indicate a bigger job. Chris will tell you honestly what the inspection shows.
